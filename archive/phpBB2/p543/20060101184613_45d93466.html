z-trip, black sheep, dj goldenchild, Sept. 1 at The Basement, Columbus, Ohio:
Z-Trip was pretty good, in his own cheesy way. brought support acts of a dj named goldenchild, who played mashups, and old-school rappers the black sheep ("the choice is yours"). it was fun to hear some of those old black sheep songs again.

z-trip played a lot of mashups (including a radiohead/tone loc bit that was pretty cool), plus some stuff from his album. he did some dvd scratching, played live drums here and there, had an mc come out for a couple tunes, and did an audience participation bit where a girl came onstage and played pac man and z-trip drummed and goldenchild cut to the video game music. problem was, the girl totally sucked at pac man, as if she'd never played it before, and kept running into a ghost about four seconds into the game play, so the bit never really got going.

the show was moved from a larger venue to a smaller one called The Basement (advertising tagline: "you've got to start somewhere") earlier in the week, and there ended up being between 60 and 65 people there. deadsville. z-trip was a good sport and still put on a good show, but i felt bad for the guy. he talked about how his label deal didn't include touring money, so he's funding the tour himself, and said that the merch sales were going to keep the gas tank full on the tour bus. my sympathy coupled with my beer-weakend resistance led me to purchase an overpriced, though pretty cool, double vinyl copy of his Uneasy Listening record.

ok, your turn...

I saw the White Stripes Saturday night at the Ohio Theater, which is an old (by U.S. standards) theater that normally is home to the local symphony orchestra and other highbrow events (though I've seen Brian Wilson and Ray Charles there in the past). It was a sold out show, and it was an interesting contrast to see a rock crowd fill up the ornate hall.

It was the third time I'd seen the White Stripes, and I'd say it was the best time yet. Partly because I like a lot of the songs on their new record, and partly because Jack White switched around on instruments a lot, playing piano, organ, guitar (duh) and even marimba. it was cool how they had microphones set up at different places around the stage so he could move around to different spots and still sing. He really is an amazing performer, a rare talent. And he's a freak. Strangely, Meg White's drumming seems to be getting worse with each tour rather than better. But I've gotten over that.

Nu Mark; Daddy G; Ooi & Kuya (Shook Daily); HiFi Bar Melbourne 24th Sept

On a post grand final whim, and conspicuously sober despite the opportunities that an AFL grand final presents in Melbourne, a last minute decision was made to head down to the HiFi to check out this excellent lineup, and it was a wise decision.

I was curious to check out that crowd that'd front up for this one, cos the cafe cool set (south of the river) would be all over massive attack's work whereas a more hip hop crowd would be more aware of nu mark's skills. And both were present...along with some of the longest dreads I'd ever seen.

Daddy G came on stage and definitely had an intense massive attack sound to his set....the dark undertones, very bassy. was quite captivating and played an amazing version of Root's Manuva's too cold (intro went for about 5 mins). His set gradually built and was getting more funky towards the end. Highlights were massive attacks unfinished sympathy (over curtis blow??? I cannot remember, but think this was the case) and Aretha belting out Rock Steady. Always brilliant.

Nu mark came on, and the man is a genius. from what I could tell, he had decks, two cdjs, drum machine, and two other things.......Wasn't close enough to see what they were, but looked like toys hooked up to a mixer/ cross fader. Was a set that ranged from old funk cuts, loads of brass, roots manuva made another appearance, some j5, some sounds from hands on, hilltop hoods nose bleed section (which got the crowd right in...the song is awesome) into some al green (fairly sure it was al green), a fair smattering of KRS1, and some men at work (land down under.....which had kuya and ooi cheering from the back of the stage (dexter/the avs have played this on several occasions in their sets too), and the crowd going nuts). Towards the end had a percussion solo, playing the lid of a record player, and a sick drum solo.

Ooi and kuya played after this, but I didn't stick around....was a little weary.

I am curious to know what the toys being played were and would appreciate it if someone could let me know

cheers

Rolling Stones and Beck, Sept 24, Nationwide Arena, Columbus

The 20,000-seat arena was sold out for the show, but fewer than half of the people were in their seats when Beck took the stage for the opening act. He did a brief set, only about 30 minutes, opening with "Loser" and going on to play "Devil's Haircut," "Where it's At," "Black Tambourine," "E-Pro" and a couple of slow acoustic ones that I assume were from his "Sea Change" album. The songs sounded good and the band seemed to be having fun, but they didn't really seem to connect with the crowd. But then, the crowd wasn't there to see Beck.

Everyone was in their seats when the Stones came on, opening with "Brown Sugar" and going on to play for just under two hours. Say what you want about people in their 60s playing rock songs, but they still sounded great and put on a very satisfying show. They really didn't look or act any older than they did the last time I saw them, in 1997.

I intentionally didn't look up any of the set lists from other shows on the tour because I didn't want to have any expectations. They played a lot of the big hits they always play when they tour, like "Honky Tonk Women," "Jumpin Jack Flash," "Satisfaction," "You Can't Always Get What You Want," "Sympathy for the Devil" and "Start me Up," but they still brought some life, and in some cases new arrangements, to the tunes, and none of them came off as just another run-through.

They also played a few tunes I wasn't expecting. "Shattered" was particularly groovy, and I really enjoyed it. Another sone I liked more than I would've guessed was "She's So Cold." They also played "Sway," from "Sticky Fingers," and Pitman's, er, Otis Redding's "Mr. Pitiful," and it was the first time they'd played either song live, Jagger said.

Seeing a band in a giant arena is never as fun as seeing one in a small club. And with most tickets costing more than $150 (plus $30 of Ticketmaster gouging), it shouldn't be surprising that the crowd was mostly middle-aged suburbanites, many of them sporting ill-fitting Stones t-shirts from tours past. But the band has so many good songs, and still plays them well, so I still had a lot of fun.
