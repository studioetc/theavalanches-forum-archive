Quote:
The fro has taken Australia and the world by storm, playing to capacity crowds and constantly hounded by screaming girls, he is in a class of his own.  Guy Sebastian is finished, move over for 4 time DMC Australian Champion and DJ extraordinaire Dexter, on of Australias finest ever DJ exports.  Having been namedropped by such powerful hip-hop figures as Chuck D from Public Enemy and self-confessed godfather of the genre Grandmaster Flash, Dexter is in no shortage of fans.


Coming to prominence as a member of The Avalanches and touring extensively around the world with the group in 2001 on the back of their critically acclaimed debut Since I left You, Dexter started to find an individual sound and style that was all his own.  Dexter returned to take on the world solo as phenomenal turntabilist talent, visiting places such as The End, Creamfields, Tribal Gathering and our very own Big Day Out and Livid festivals along the way.  His shows are anything if eclectic, drawing on straight up Hip-hop, 80s pop, electro, drumnbass, breaks and everything in between.  Blending what on paper seems a clash of styles and sounds into a an utterly convincing and consistent whole is what Dexter does best, hearing Jimmy Hendrixs Purple Haze alongside Roni Sizes Child of the Wild West is never uncommon nor out of place in a Dexter set.  

Dexter took some time out to have a chat with us ahead of a road trip this weekend down the Mornington Peninsula to give Frankstonians a taste of the action this Friday night.   

Q. Ill start off with the question on everyones lips.  Whats the word on your long awaited full debut release?  Can you give an indication of what to expect from the CD?

A. Still working away, I'd like to think It'll be finished soon.  I'd also like to think I don't lie.  Im trying to create something almost exotic, a sound that combines cultures and moods, a similar feeling to traveling, like when I discover a new place.

Essentially the record is international funk,soul & hiphop, aiming to combine a handful of favourite songs with my own production.

Q. Its been a big year for Dexter, with your split from the Avalanches and  playing to massive crowds at festivals such as Meredith and The Falls.  How do you personally rate the live experience against that of the long hours in the studio?

A. I like both. The most difficult task has been to create the same feeling live as in the studio. Understanding the difference between live DJ sets and studio DJ sets, listening and feeling my way through ideas has been a good learning process.

Q. The scene of the sun rising through the gum trees over a few thousand happy dancing campers dancing is an image that will stick in many peoples minds from your set at Meredith late last year.  Do large outdoor festivals such as the Meredith Music Festival hold something special for you?

A. Yes, I've never experienced a festival like that before, that was some once in a lifetime shit. Too bad for those who missed it.

Q. Dropping Curtis Mayfields Move on Up to the dismay of the many gathered festival officials trying to cut your already overlong set was an incredible moment last year at Meredith, showing the passion you give to your live sets..  Do you feel a real connection with the crowd throughout the couple of hours your behind the decks?

A. I don't change my set according to "the crowd". What does that really mean anyway? All the records I play are straight from my heart.  I love each one, so (when I play) I'm giving a part of me to you; I'm putting myself on the line. To get a response like I did at Meredith, fills me with happiness.

Q. If sell out shows for Kid Koala, RJD2 and many others recently are anything to go by, is it fair to say that the turntabalism/scratching/beats scene is enjoying something of a renaissance at the moment?  Are you encouraged by the level of support shown by local promoters for artists such as yourself and visiting international acts?

A. There are only so many turtablists that can come out and keep it interesting to experience.  Kid Koala was the most special turntablist performance I've seen. Sound is his thing, technical ability is just a means to an end; he sounded incredible.

The support is fantastic; Melbourne has blossomed into an internationally recognized music city, thanks to the support.

Q. Youve always seemed to go out of your way to play gigs in regional areas, over the past few years given places like Ballarat, Geelong and Tasmania as part of the Falls Festival the full Dexter treatment.  Do you think that its important that the more out of the way places get more exposure from DJs and the like?

A. The regional areas are quite beautiful, so why would the people wanna leave?  Yes, the city has a lot to offer, but so can we as artists, we should make the effort to come out to regional areas, build up these areas as places not just for a fun night out, but to give inspiration, to give something unique.

This is why I enjoy Europe because I can play 40 shows in the U.K.alone whilst only playing once or twice in London.

Q. Can you comment on a rumor that you were chased out of Ballarat by police after playing well after closing time at a club gig there last year?

A. Who can catch me?  I'll out run a coppa anytime.

Q. The Prince of Wales in Melbourne could almost be called your spiritual home, having played some memorable nights there over the years in whats almost becoming a yearly event not to miss.  Is this a venue that holds special significance for you?  Any chance of another show this year from you and your brother Kuya?

A. Its such a great room, we've managed to utilise it differently by setting up a club atmosphere.  Its special cos it was the (first) "proper" gig venue I've headlined, sold out both shows too. Nice one. I'll be doing a lot of recording this year, and working on a new live set; which is taking time.  So not really playing much just now, I do have Canberra, Jindabyne, Wollongong and Brisbane in the next few months.

I'd like to re-unite the 2 minute needles
1.  So we can change our name
2. Cos we were on something special before we even started.

Q. In the opening of many of your sets there is an extended section where you play an old waltz style track interlaced with a very phat beat mixed in usually accompanied by an old B&W visual on the screen which knocks me over every time.  Can you tell me what you are doing here and where you got the inspiration for this section from?

A. The old waltz is from the Annie get your Gun sountrack, I produced it in 1999 for the DMC comp. I forgot about it for years, until recently and included it in the live set.  It still sounds so fresh and so clean-clean to this day.

Q. How do you see the explosion of the hip-hop scene in the past 6 months in Australia, in particular the Hilltop Hoods mainstream breakthrough with The Nosebleed Section.  Can you look into your crystal ball and see this as a continuing trend in the market or just a bubble ready to burst?

A. What ever it is I'm hoping the quality gets better.  With the exception of a few creating some solid beats, all I hear on the radio is Dr.Dre, Premier & Kanye West rip offs. I hope Australian hip-hop continues to grow.

Q. Do you still keep in touch with your old Avalanches crew?  Any word on future plans for them, a rumored upcoming full-length release?

A. They're working on their next record.  Im not sure when they've scheduled the release.

Q. Youve just returned from a short stint in Japan.  What feedback did you get from audiences over there?  Japan seems almost a hotspot for turntabalists these days, with domestic talent such as DJ Crush known worldwide. 

A. It was excellent.  It was for the launch of a new store called BBS (BreakBeatScience), a records, music lifestyle store. Several well-known artists created a model city complete with a train system, a glass floor covered the model, so you could stand over it and look above. The train was fitted with a camera, which was sending a signal to a flat screen in front of the shop so passers by could see what was going on.

Tokyo at its best. The gig was in store, no pressure, nice n easy for once.

It was a holiday with a little work involved.

Q. Are there any players on the local scene at the moment who stick out to you as important or particularly influential on your style?

A. Im influenced by few DJ's, Kid Koala made me re-think turntablism and where it can go. Im always searching for inspiration, Femi Kuti at the Prince was incredible, blew me away completely!  Who needs drugs.

Q. If you were stuck on a desert island which 7 albums would you take with you and why?

A. Can I say 7 shittest albums of all time. Cos I'd go retarded and it wouldn't matter anymore.

Q. What should people heading down to Frankston this Friday expect from your ever-evolving live show?  There is word of surprise special guests.  Any tracks that have caught your ear recently that might get a run on the night?  Is there any sibling rivalry when you and your big bro Kuya share the decks?

A. No rivalry.... Expect some special guests during my set to blow you away.  I think they're gonna blow me away.  New records?  I wish.  I'm looking for love in all the wrong places, sometimes record digging is like pissing in the wind.

Dexter plays this Friday 22nd May - 21st Century Nightclub- Frankston with support from Kuya, DJ Grotesque and DJ FX.
