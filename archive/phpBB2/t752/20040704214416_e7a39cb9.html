good idea, getting a turntable

if your "stereo" (i'm assuming it's a receiver) has a phono line in, you can plug a turntable directly into that, no problem. the turntable also will have a ground wire and your receiver should have a little screw thing where you put the ground wire so the turntable is grounded. if it's not grounded, you'll get a lot of hum.

if your receiver doesn't have a phono line in, you probably should get a phono preamp. see, the signal from a turntable is lower (i.e. quieter) than the signal from cd players, tape decks, tuners, mini disk players, etc., so a phono input has a built-in signal boost. if you plug a turntable into a cd input, it will play very quietly. if you have a powerful amp i suppose you could just crank up the volume (remember to turn it down before you play a cd or other media so you don't get blasted). a phono preamp would be a little box that would boost the signal before it gets to your receiver, so the records would play at the same volume as everything else. (you also could use a dj mixer as a preamp, but that's an expensive way to go if you're only using it as a preamp and not doing any mixing.)

i do have a portable vestax and that might be a decent option for you. the built-in mono speaker is so-so, but the stereo line out (as well as the stereo headphones jack) provides great sound, and you wouldn't need a preamp or phono input with it, as the vestax signal is the same as cd, etc.

thing is, the portable vestax is pretty lightweight and doesn't seem to be the most durable piece of equipment. i don't know how it would do under heavy use on a regular basis. if the portability wouldn't be an advantage for you, i'd opt for a regular turntable instead.

there are two types of turntables, belt drive and direct drive. the belt drive models rotate the platter using a belt, and the direct drive models rotate the platter using gears directly. at the high (i.e. expensive) end, belt drives provide better sound quality because they dampen vibration more BUT you can't scratch with a belt drive model because it would stretch the belt and cause it so play at inconsistent speeds, giving you a warbly effect. so if you plan to fiddle around with scratching, definitely go for a direct drive model (the portable Vestax is belt driven).

hope this helps. if you have more questions, just ask.
